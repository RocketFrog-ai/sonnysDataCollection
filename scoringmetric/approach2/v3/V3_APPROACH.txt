================================================================================
  CAR WASH QUANTILE PREDICTION — V3 APPROACH
  Simple explanation and methodological example
================================================================================


WHAT THE SYSTEM DOES
--------------------

For any new car wash location, the system does four things:

  1. Predicts which performance tier the site will likely fall into:
     Q1 (bottom 25%), Q2, Q3, or Q4 (top 25%) by annual car wash volume.

  2. Shows the expected volume range (e.g. Q4: 174,000–479,000 cars per year).

  3. Compares each feature (weather, distance to stores, competition, site age,
     tunnel count, etc.) to what high-performing (Q4) sites typically have,
     and labels them as strengths or weaknesses.

  4. Summarises in plain language: a short narrative and an investment-style
     strengths and weaknesses section.

All tiers and benchmarks come from real data: 482 sites with both features
and car wash counts. No guesswork or made-up numbers.


HOW WE DEFINE “TOP” AND “BOTTOM” (QUARTILES)
--------------------------------------------

  • We sort all 482 sites by car wash count, from lowest to highest.

  • We split them into four equal-sized groups (about 121 sites each):
      Q1 = bottom 25% (lowest volume)
      Q2 = next 25%
      Q3 = next 25%
      Q4 = top 25% (highest volume)

  • So “Q4” always means “in the top 25% of sites by volume.” The exact
    count ranges come from the data. In our dataset they are roughly:
      Q1: 47 – 57,846 cars/year
      Q2: 57,846 – 107,252 cars/year
      Q3: 107,252 – 174,047 cars/year
      Q4: 174,047 – 478,789 cars/year


HOW WE FOUND OUT THE IMPORTANCE OF EACH FEATURE
-----------------------------------------------

  • We train a Random Forest model: 27 features in, quartile (Q1–Q4) out.
    The model learns which features help most to separate low vs high
    performers.

  • “Importance” of a feature = how much that feature is used in the
    tree splits and how much it reduces prediction error. The model
    reports a share for each feature (e.g. tunnel count 31.9%, site age
    4.2%). These add up to 100%. So we know which features the model
    relies on most when predicting your quartile.

  • We do not assign importance by hand. It comes directly from the
    trained model on the 482 sites.


THE PERCENTILE WAY: YOUR VALUE VS ALL SITES AND VS Q4
-----------------------------------------------------

  For each feature we do three things:

  (1) YOUR RAW PERCENTILE
      We take your value and ask: what share of all 482 sites have a value
      lower than yours? That is your raw percentile (0–100%).
      Example: Your tunnel count is 3. So we count how many sites have
      tunnel count less than 3. If 93% of sites have less than 3, your
      raw percentile is 93% — you are above 93% of sites on this feature.

  (2) DIRECTION: HIGHER IS BETTER OR LOWER IS BETTER
      For some features (e.g. tunnel count, pleasant days), higher is
      better for car wash performance. For others (e.g. distance to
      Walmart, snowfall), lower is better.
      So we fix “good” to mean “toward better performance”:
        • Higher is better: raw percentile 93% means you are better than
          93% of sites → strong.
        • Lower is better: raw percentile 90% means 90% of sites have a
          lower value than you, so you have a high (bad) value → we show
          “adjusted percentile” = 100 − 90 = 10%, meaning you are only
          better than 10% of sites on the “good” scale.

  (3) Q4 MEDIAN: WHAT TOP PERFORMERS TYPICALLY HAVE
      For each feature we compute the median value among Q4 (top 25%)
      sites. So we know “what does a high-performing site typically
      have for this feature?”
      In the report we show: Your value, your percentile (or adjusted
      percentile), and the Q4 median. So you can read:
        • “My value is X; Q4 sites typically have Y. So I am above/below
          the Q4 benchmark.”
      Example: Your competitor review count is 145. Q4 median is 220.5.
      So you are below what top performers typically have — that is a
      weakness. Another example: Your nearest competitor is 0.2 miles.
      Q4 median is 0.5 miles. For distance, lower is better, so 0.2
      is better than 0.5 — you exceed the Q4 benchmark.


METHODOLOGY EXAMPLE: PERCENTILE AND Q4 COMPARISON
-------------------------------------------------

  Feature: Distance to nearest competitor (miles). Lower is better.

  Your value: 0.2 miles

  Step 1 — Raw percentile
    Among all 482 sites, we rank 0.2. Say 82% of sites have a distance
    greater than 0.2 (i.e. they are farther from a competitor). So your
    raw percentile is 82%: you are closer than 82% of sites.

  Step 2 — Direction (lower is better)
    For “distance,” lower is better. So being at 82nd percentile (you have
    a lower value than 82% of sites) is good. We do not invert. Your
    “adjusted” percentile for reporting = 82% — you are better than 82%
    of sites on this feature.

  Step 3 — Q4 median
    Among Q4 (top 25% by car wash volume) sites, the median distance to
    nearest competitor is 0.5 miles. Your 0.2 is lower than 0.5, so you
    exceed what Q4 sites typically have. So we label this a strength:
    “matches Q4” or “exceeds Q4.”

  Summary line in the report:
    Your value 0.2, your percentile 82%, Q4 median 0.5 → you are better
    than the Q4 benchmark (strength).

  Another example: Gas station rating. Higher is better.
    Your value: 3.4. Say your raw percentile is 22% (only 22% of sites
    have a lower rating). So you are below most sites. Q4 median is 4.0.
    Your 3.4 is below 4.0, so you are below what Q4 sites typically have
    → weakness.


HOW WE COMPARE FEATURES (WASHQ)
-------------------------------

  • For each feature we look at the median value of that feature in each
    quartile (Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4) of car wash performance.

  • For a new location we compare its value to those medians. If it is
    closer to what Q4 sites have, we say it “matches Q4” or “exceeds Q4.”
    If it is closer to Q1, we say it “matches Q1.”

  • We only assign this “WashQ” label for features that have a meaningful
    correlation with car wash count (above a signal threshold). Others are
    marked as low signal so we do not over-interpret noise.


TUNNEL COUNT AND EFFECTIVE CAPACITY
------------------------------------

  • Tunnel count is a site-capacity estimate from 1 to 4, derived from
    annual volume for existing sites:

      Under 1.2 lakh (120,000) cars/year   →  1 tunnel
      1.2–2.4 lakh                         →  2 tunnels
      2.4–3.6 lakh                         →  3 tunnels
      3.6 lakh and above                   →  4 tunnels

  • For a new site you can supply the actual tunnel count (1–4);
    if you do not know it, the model fills it from similar sites.

  • Effective Capacity = tunnel_count × is_Express
    Only Express Tunnel sites have physical conveyor tunnels. Mobile washes,
    Hand Wash / Detail, Flex and other types do not — so for them, effective
    capacity is 0 regardless of their tunnel count proxy.
    This is the 2nd most important feature (importance 16.2%, r=+0.74).

  • Why this matters: a low-volume Express site with 1 tunnel (tunnel=1,
    effective_capacity=1) is very different from a Mobile or Hand Wash site
    (tunnel=1, effective_capacity=0). Without effective_capacity the model
    cannot separate them; with it, accuracy rose from 62.4% to 62.9%.


SITE AGE
--------

  • Site age (years in operation) is the second most important feature.
    Lower age tends to go with better performance: newer sites have
    ramp-up momentum and more modern operations.

  • Age is joined from the site master so all 482 sites have it.


MODEL AND ACCURACY
------------------

  • The model is a Random Forest classifier: 400 trees, 28 features, with
    calibration so the reported probabilities are reliable. Missing values
    are filled using the 5 nearest similar sites (KNN imputation).

  • Accuracy (5-fold cross-validation):
      Without tunnel count:  37% exact quartile, 73% within one quartile.
      With tunnel count:     62.9% exact quartile, 97.9% within one quartile.

  • “Within one quartile” means the prediction is either exactly right or
    off by just one tier (e.g. we say Q4 and the true tier is Q3). So with
    tunnel count, almost every site is predicted in the right tier or the
    tier next to it.


WHAT IS IN THE REPORT
---------------------

  1. Predicted quartile (Q1–Q4) and expected volume range.
  2. Prediction confidence: probability bars for each quartile.
  3. Feature table: all 27 features with value, WashQ, percentile, Q4 median,
     signal strength, and importance. Values filled from similar sites are
     marked as imputed.
  4. Strengths and weaknesses: investment-style summary (LLM-generated).
  5. Shift opportunities: which features, if improved, could move the site
     to a better quartile (when not already top tier).
  6. Profile comparison: your value vs the median in each quartile for
     key features.
  7. Narrative summary: short prose (LLM-generated).
  8. Footer: exact and within-one-quartile accuracy.

There are no error messages or fallback placeholders in the report text.


================================================================================
  METHODOLOGICAL EXAMPLE (END-TO-END)
================================================================================

Suppose we have a new car wash location and we want to know how it will
perform compared to all existing sites.

STEP 1 — INPUT
  We have (or we derive) 27 numbers for this site, for example:
    • Weather: rainy days 119, pleasant temp days 150, snowfall 47.7 cm, etc.
    • Distance to Walmart 2.3 miles, to Target 2.1 miles, to nearest
      competitor 0.2 miles.
    • Site age 2 years, tunnel count 3, region and state encoded.
    • Gas station rating 3.4, competitor review count 145, and so on.
  If some values are missing (e.g. we do not know distance to Target), the
  model fills them from the 5 most similar sites in the dataset.

STEP 2 — QUARTILE PREDICTION
  The model takes these 27 features and predicts which quartile this site
  belongs to. Suppose it predicts Q4 (top 25%) with 87% confidence. So we
  say: “This site is projected to be a Q4 high performer, with expected
  annual volume between 174,047 and 478,789 cars.”

STEP 3 — PERCENTILE AND Q4 COMPARISON FOR EACH FEATURE
  For each feature we report:
    • Your value.
    • Your percentile (or adjusted percentile if “lower is better”): what
      share of all sites you are better than on this feature.
    • Q4 median: the typical value among top 25% car wash sites.
  So you can see at a glance: “My value is X; I am better than Y% of sites;
  Q4 sites typically have Z. So I am above/below the Q4 benchmark.” WashQ
  (matches Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4) is our summary of that comparison.

STEP 4 — STRENGTHS AND WEAKNESSES
  From the WashQ and values we build a list of strengths (features at or
  above Q4 level) and weaknesses (features at Q1/Q2 level). An LLM then
  turns this into two short paragraphs: one on strengths, one on
  weaknesses, in investment-report style.

STEP 5 — NARRATIVE
  The same LLM writes a 2–3 sentence summary: predicted quartile, expected
  volume range, and 1–2 standout features (e.g. tunnel count and age exceed
  Q4; competitor review count falls short).

STEP 6 — SHIFT OPPORTUNITIES (IF NOT ALREADY Q4)
  If the site is predicted Q1, Q2, or Q3, we ask: “If we improved one
  feature at a time to the next quartile’s typical level, which feature
  would most likely move the prediction up?” We report the top such
  opportunities. If the site is already Q4, we say there are no shift
  opportunities.

OUTPUT
  The user gets a single report: predicted tier, confidence, full feature
  table (your value, your percentile, Q4 median, WashQ, importance),
  strengths and weaknesses text, profile comparison, narrative, and (when
  relevant) shift opportunities — in simple terms.
